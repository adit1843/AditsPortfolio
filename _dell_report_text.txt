Executive Summary
This report outlines the completion of the Dell Direct Liquid Cooling (DLC) senior design
project, sponsored by Dell Technologies. The project’s objective is to improve the reliability of
DLC systems in data centers by creating a robust and scalable solution to isolate coolant leaks
and mitigate damage stemming from system failures. With data centers expanding to meet
growing computational demands, ensuring consistent and efficient cooling is more critical than
ever.
Given the initial open-ended nature of the project scope, the team collaborated closely with Dell
and course instructors to define goals and design requirements. These requirements emphasized
constraints such as space, scalability, and ease of maintenance. The team then designed a
prototype consisting of four modules designed to work together to isolate leaks within a rack
server. The actuator module employs a rack-and-pinion mechanism powered by a servo motor to
disconnect quick-disconnect (QD) valves, effectively stopping coolant flow to areas where leaks
have been detected. This actuator is mounted on the elevator module, allowing it to move
vertically across server levels. The prototype uses a screw elevator for its compact form factor
and reliability. The water circulation module simulates coolant flow to rack servers using
off-the-shelf pumps, manifolds, and hoses. An Arduino-based control module manages system
operations, utilizing a limit switch to zero the elevator and allowing for manual overrides to
ensure both functionality and safety. All components are housed within a modular 8020
aluminum frame for structural integrity and ease of assembly.
After completing the embodiment design phase, the team worked to assemble the prototype
while overcoming challenges such as material delays and necessary design revisions. Key
changes included replacing welded QD collars with shaft collars to protect ball bearings from
heat damage and reinforcing the actuator housing for improved rigidity. The team also applied
Finite Element Analysis (FEA) to validate structural strength and minimize deflection within the
cantilever beam used to connect the actuator module and the elevator module.
The team utilized risk assessment methods such as failure modes and effects analysis (FMEA)
and fault tree analysis (FTA) to identify key failure modes, such as actuator failures, elevator
misalignment, and power loss. These methods led to the implementation of durable servo motors
and reinforced mechanical components. The validation plan includes performance metrics such
as a 10-second actuation time and an under-2mm frame deflection under a 50 lb load. The project
remains within its $2,667 prototype budget, with a projected unit cost of $528, which could be
further reduced through Dell’s bulk purchasing.
Following the completion of the prototype and presentation at the Engineering Project
Showcase event, the team must deliver the prototype to Dell headquarters.

1. Introduction and Problem Definition
As technology continues to evolve, data servers have become vital across industries, supporting
business operations, communication, and data storage. With rising computational demands, the
need for effective cooling solutions has grown. Traditional air cooling methods are becoming
insufficient, prompting innovations like immersion cooling—where servers are submerged in
dielectric fluids—and two-phase cooling, which uses boiling liquid to remove heat from
components. These approaches aim to maintain server performance and prevent failures caused
by overheating.
Dell, a global leader in technology solutions, provides complete server systems and infrastructure
to businesses. To support their commitment to efficient and reliable data center operations, Dell
partnered with a team of five senior mechanical engineering students from Texas A&M to
investigate and improve their Direct Liquid Cooling (DLC) system. The team—Adit Sanghani,
Noah Sterenback, Wilson Ramsey, Danny Hoang, and Juan Lafon—was tasked with developing
an innovative solution to enhance system reliability by addressing potential failure modes.
Through site visits to Dell’s headquarters and the Texas Advanced Computing Center, the team
gained first-hand insight into DLC system operations and failure points. The DLC system
includes a cooling distribution unit, manifolds, and cold plates, all connected by tubing with
quick disconnects (QDs). While current leak-detection systems shut down the entire rack upon
identifying a leak, this approach is inefficient and overly cautious when only a single server level
is affected.
After background research, the team developed the following problem statement:
“Design a holistic solution capable of tracking physical metrics within the liquid cooling
system and preserving component health during failures.”
This guided the team toward a solution focused on improving reliability and minimizing
downtime.
The team identified key customer needs based on research and input from sponsors as
summarized in Table 1 below. These needs were then rated by importance. Given the tight space
in server racks and the diversity of customer systems, the solution had to be compact, modular,
and easy to integrate. It also needed to be cost-effective and robust to minimize maintenance and
improve uptime.

2. System Description
The final system is a modular, automated prototype designed to replicate the mechanical and
fluid handling operations found in liquid-cooled server environments. It consists of four
integrated subsystems: an actuator mechanism for bidirectional quick disconnect operation, a
vertical elevator for alignment and engagement, a closed-loop water circulation system, and a
control system based on the ESP32 microcontroller. These modules are mounted within a
compact prototype frame built using 8020 extrusions.
The actuator is mounted on the elevator platform using a steel bracket made from bent sheet
metal and reinforced with welded gussets. This platform moves vertically along aluminum rails
driven by a lead screw mechanism, aligning the actuator with the quick disconnect collars
connected to the water manifolds. Water flows through the system via rigid tubing connected to
intake and output manifolds, forming a closed circulation loop. The entire system is governed by
the control subsystem, which accepts user input to disconnect certain levels of the prototype.

The actuator subsystem is responsible for physically engaging and disengaging the quick
disconnects. A rack and pinion mechanism, enclosed in a 3D-printed housing, translates the
rotary motion of a servo motor into linear motion in both directions. The rack and pinion was
chosen for its mechanical simplicity and ability to operate within the limited space of the
prototype. Additionally, the rack and pinion components were easily purchasable online, and an
additional mechanical face interacting with the QD’s was welded to the ends of the pinions. The
housing was 3D-printed due to its complex geometry requiring advanced manufacturing
techniques if it were created using harder materials such as steel or aluminum. Figure 3 and 4
shows general schematics of the actuator subsystem.

Supporting the actuator, the elevator subsystem provides vertical motion to align the actuator
with the manifold-mounted quick disconnects. A stepper motor drives a lead screw that translates
the actuator assembly along linear guide rails. This subsystem was chosen over belt-driven
design for its precision and self-locking characteristics. Upon startup, the elevator zeroes itself
using a limit switch at the bottom of its travel, establishing a consistent baseline for all
subsequent movements. This homing process significantly improved the repeatability of
disconnect operations. Figure 5 presents the general schematic of the elevator subsystem.

The water circulation subsystem comprises two manifolds—one for fluid intake and the other for
output—connected via rigid tubing and quick disconnects (QDs). Each QD features a detachable
collar designed to enlarge the interface between the QD and the actuator, ensuring consistent and
reliable engagement and disengagement. An early design proposal involved welding washers to
the QDs to increase the actuation surface area; however, after consultation with staff at the Fisher
Engineering Design Center, this approach was abandoned due to concerns that the heat from
welding could compromise the integrity of the internal ball bearings. The selected rigid tubing
offers a secure, kink-resistant flow path between connection points, enhancing reliability and
repeatability during operation. To support system monitoring, a flowmeter is integrated into the
intake line, allowing users to track real-time flow rate and internal pressure. Figure 6 provides a
schematic overview of the water circulation subsystem.

Functionality
The current design successfully meets the primary customer requirements by providing a
fail-safe mechanism for isolating coolant flow in Dell's Direct Liquid Cooling (DLC) system.
The product architecture consists of a rack-and-pinion mechanism, quick disconnect (QD)
valves, and a belt-pulley system for vertical travel. This architecture ensures that the system
operates efficiently within existing server racks while maintaining modularity for future
scalability. The stipulated function of the system—to act as mitigation against liquid coolant

Working Principles and Form Solutions
The design relies on mechanical actuation through a rack-and-pinion mechanism to

The system design incorporates modular T-slotted aluminum framing for easy assembly
and adaptability. The cantilever arm and rack-and-pinion housing underwent modifications to
improve rigidity, with thickness increased from 2 mm to 6 mm at key load-bearing points and
infill increased from 50% to 80% in 3D-printed components. These changes significantly
reduced deflection while maintaining a lightweight structure.

required torque while requiring small amounts of power. This system is extremely efficient
because the only energy loss will be due to frictional elements. All motion is directly translated
from rotational to linear motion.

Safety Considerations
The system is designed to return to a zero position when not in use, keeping it out of the
way to prevent interference with normal operations. That way the actuator can't affect any
pre-existing components already there if it malfunctions. Electrical components operate at a
maximum of 24 volts and 2 amps, ensuring low power requirements and reducing potential
electrical hazards. Additionally, all materials are corrosion-resistant, ensuring longevity in
high-moisture environments. The system will also have its own separate manual to allow for
operators to better understand how it works.
Ergonomics
The design avoids unnecessary complexity, ensuring that all interactions are intuitive and
require minimal training. Additionally, the system is structured to be compact and unobtrusive,
reducing spatial constraints in data center environments. User manuals will be provided to reduce
stress from confusion on operators.
Production and Quality Control
The system's production process has been optimized for cost-efficiency and
manufacturability, with primary components sourced from established vendors rather than
manufactured. The waterjet cutting process ensures precision and repeatability in manufacturing
cantilever arms, while preassembled quick disconnect collars reduce fabrication complexity.
Quality control measures include tolerance checks on all machined components and verification
of actuation reliability before deployment.
Assembly and Maintenance
The modular nature of the system ensures ease of assembly, with all major components
attached via bolted connections rather than permanent fixtures. The use of T-slotted gussets
allows for adjustable mounting, simplifying alignment with the manifold. Maintenance
procedures are straightforward, allowing for quick inspections and part replacements without
extensive downtime.

Transport Considerations
The system is designed for easy transport, with all components capable of being
disassembled and reassembled with minimal effort. Packaging considerations include protective
foam inserts to prevent damage during shipment. Additionally, all components are lightweight,
minimizing handling risks. The housing containing the rack and pinion could be protected with
foam and with a few simple wire connections the system can be ready for operation.
Lifecycle and Cost Considerations
The system is designed for long-term use, with minimal maintenance required beyond
periodic inspections. A simple running of the system once a month would be sufficient in testing
if components need repair. The decision to purchase off-the-shelf parts and prebuilt elevator
components reduced both manufacturing complexity and cost. While cost is not the primary
constraint, minimizing expenses where possible remains beneficial. Dell’s ability to source
materials at lower costs further enhances feasibility for large-scale implementation. The design
prioritizes reliability over cost, recognizing that avoiding server damage from coolant leaks is the
key financial advantage.

The project remains on track to meet expected delivery deadlines, with all major

modifications to reduce cycle time include pre-assembling subsystems before final installation
and refining alignment procedures to decrease setup time.

methods, and modularity, the system remains cost-effective, reliable, and adaptable. The design
is now prepared for validation testing, where further refinements may be made based on
real-world performance evaluations.

3.1.2 FMCA
Risk analysis is a crucial component of the design process, where the project team
systematically identifies and evaluates potential risks that could impact the project's success.
This process involves assessing the likelihood and severity of each risk, enabling the team to
prioritize those that pose the greatest threat. Preventative measures and mitigation strategies will
be developed and implemented to reduce the chances of these risks occurring or minimize their
impact should they arise. By conducting a thorough risk analysis, the design team can
proactively address potential challenges and uncertainties, leading to more informed
decision-making and a smoother project cycle. This foresight allows for better planning, resource
allocation, and contingency strategies, ultimately ensuring that the project stays on track and
meets its goals with greater efficiency and safety.
The team conducted a Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA) to identify potential
risks associated with the prototype design, assess their likelihood of occurrence, evaluate their
severity on the project's outcomes, and determine how easily these failures could be detected
after they occurred. Five key components within the prototype design were identified as having
potential failure modes: the QD actuator, elevator, QD collar, electrical control system, and
frame support. Once the potential failure modes were identified, each was assigned ratings across
three categories: severity, occurrence, and detection. Severity indicated the level of impact a
failure would have on the overall success of the project, with higher scores reflecting more
detrimental consequences. Occurrence represented the likelihood of the failure mode happening,
with higher scores indicating a greater chance of occurrence. Detection scores assessed how
difficult it would be to identify the failure after it happened, with higher scores indicating that the
failure would be harder to detect.
Once each failure mode was assigned a score in the three categories, the values were
multiplied together to obtain the Risk Priority Number (RPN). The RPN serves as a measure of
how critical a failure mode is to the success of the project. Failure modes with higher RPN
values were considered more critical and prioritized for corrective action. These high-RPN
failure modes received special attention to ensure that mitigation strategies and preventative
measures could be implemented, reducing the overall risk to the project's success.

After calculating the failure modes and their respective RPNs, recommended courses of
action were developed for each failure mode to provide clear guidance in case the failure occurs

study gave the team insight on how to address potential failure modes was when the motor

3.1.3 FTA
In addition to conducting a Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA), the design team
also employed Fault Tree Analysis (FTA) to further assess potential risks and failure modes
within the project. The FTA diagram begins with a top-level primary failure event that the team
seeks to understand and prevent, followed by a branching structure that connects to underlying
events that could contribute to the top-level failure. The diagram incorporates AND/OR logic
gates to help illustrate how different events interact and lead to the failure. This tool helped the
team identify critical failure modes, develop effective mitigation strategies, and enhance the
overall system reliability. An example of this analysis was carried out on the elevator component
shown in the figure below. While the fault tree diagram is valuable for understanding the

Despite the limitations of the FTA diagrams for our prototypes, it was still valuable to visualize

3.2 Design For Manufacturing and Design For Assembly
Our design process incorporated several Design for Manufacturing (DFM) and Design for
Assembly (DFA) strategies to streamline prototype development and inform future production
considerations.
Design for Manufacturing (DFM)
20

-- 21 of 34 --

We made deliberate design decisions to reduce complexity and cost in manufacturing. One
example is the rack and pinion system, which replaced an earlier concept involving two separate
mechanisms. By integrating two racks and a single pinion, we significantly reduced the part
count and simplified the system.
The cantilever beam was custom-designed but straightforward to fabricate using sheet metal. Our
process used waterjet cutting and a single bend operation, followed by welded supports to reduce
deflection. If a thicker sheet metal is used, welding could be eliminated entirely—simplifying the
process further.
The housing posed the most significant manufacturing challenge. For the prototype, we 3D
printed this part, but its geometric features make it a strong candidate for injection molding in
production, where mold costs are justifiable.
Our elevator track and elevator platform are made from standard, off-the-shelf components.
While the track required no modifications, the elevator platform includes many small parts,
making it difficult to automate. A future redesign could dramatically improve manufacturability
and reduce complexity.
Design for Assembly (DFA)
We standardized fasteners across the system, which simplifies both manual and automated
assembly. Most parts are intuitive to put together, with clear interfaces, and the system can be
easily mounted to a rack cabinet. We also ensured that it’s straightforward to disassemble
individual parts for replacement or recycling.
That said, we identified several opportunities for improving assembly. Some components—like
the elevator platform—could benefit from a redesign to reduce the number of small parts and
tedious sub-assemblies. We also noted that attaching the cantilever beam to the platform is
time-consuming and could be optimized with a better joining strategy.
In future iterations, we recommend incorporating asymmetry into certain parts to prevent
incorrect orientation during assembly, and simplifying servo mounting to reduce labor time and
potential for error.

4. Broader Impacts of Design
As the project concludes, it is important to consider the broader impacts of the design beyond its
immediate application. In addition to improving reliability in data centers, the system has wider
implications across industry, safety, automation, and sustainability. This section also discusses
lifecycle design considerations and potential intellectual property opportunities associated with
the final product.
The quick disconnect actuator system developed in this project has potential applications across
a wide range of industries. While originally designed for server rack cooling systems, the core
24

-- 25 of 34 --

concept can be adapted for manufacturing, automotive, aerospace, HVAC, and medical systems,
anywhere that fluid quick disconnects are commonly used. The design directly improves data
center protection by mitigating the risks of coolant leaks, reducing hardware damage, and
minimizing costly downtime.
The system also introduces significant cost savings. Downtime for a single 42U server rack is
estimated to cost about $80,000 for just four hours, and the losses continue to rise the longer the
system is offline. By enabling faster leak isolation and reducing the need for complete
shutdowns, the design has the potential to save thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars in
equipment protection and service continuity. From a safety perspective, the ability to remotely or
automatically disconnect fluid lines reduces the need for human intervention, limiting exposure
to hazardous fluids and improving workplace safety standards.
In terms of future readiness, the design supports the ongoing shift toward automation by enabling
intelligent leak response without the need for constant human monitoring. This allows
maintenance personnel to focus on more critical tasks and improves overall operational
efficiency. Additionally, the design contributes to sustainability efforts by reducing electronic
waste associated with hardware failures. It relies heavily on off-the-shelf and reusable parts,
making repair and replacement easier while minimizing the environmental impact.
4.1 Lifecycle Design
Lifecycle considerations played an important role in both the prototype and the envisioned
production version of the system. Material selection focused on durability and environmental
responsibility. The design uses corrosion resistant metals like steel and aluminum, which are
recyclable and extend the operational life of the system. Wherever possible, fasteners were
chosen instead of welded joints. This decision reduces manufacturing complexity, allows easier
repairs, and supports material separation at the end of life.
During usage and maintenance, the modular structure of the system allows for quick part
replacement with minimal downtime. Standardized components simplify future repairs, making
the system easier to service without specialized tools. The use of a low voltage control system
also reduces overall energy consumption during operation, lowering the environmental footprint
over time.
At the end of the system's service life, the design supports easy disassembly and recycling.
Electronic components can be removed separately, minimizing electronic waste and making
responsible disposal more practical. The system can be refurbished through simple component
swaps or, if still functional, relocated to another rack even after the original server it protected is
retired. Potential future programs could also include manufacturer take back options or

4.2 Intellectual Property
Early in our conversations with our Dell sponsors this semester, they mentioned the
possibility of submitting for intellectual property in the form of a patent for our project. As the
semester progressed, we submitted documents to the Dell team and met with one of their lawyers
to give them a better idea of the project, and parts of the system that is worth including in the
patent request. As of now, they are still processing it all internally, but there is a high likelihood
that it will become patent pending in the upcoming months. The specific IP is protecting a
node-level server isolation solution that utilizes a vertical travel quick disconnect actuation
device. It is broken into two modules: The vertical travel module and the actuation module. It
receives an outside indicator of a leak from the preexisting leak detectors, and sends a request to
move the actuation module via the vertical travel module to the correct level from the leak
indicator. The system then actuates the leakless quick disconnect, stopping the flow to the
individual server. The patent contains embodiments for different methods of vertical travel and
actuation than the lead screw elevator and rack and pinion system we utilized for our prototype.
This includes belt, rack and pinion, scissor lift, and other vertical travel mechanisms as well as
linear solenoid, cam and follower, and other actuation mechanisms. Overall, we are excited to
potentially be named on a Dell patent, and that it contains multiple embodiments of our design.
4.3 Liability Considerations
A lot of liability for both the prototype and real-life integration of our solution stems
from human error. If that is the case of product misuse, misinstallation, or manufacturing errors,
the result is a problem that can cause Dell potential problems. To mitigate these issues, there are
a number of steps that can be taken to remove the potential liability of the product. The first is
creating proper user instructions and teaching user safety for the product. This means proper
documentation, user manuals, and troubleshooting guides are needed to ensure no misuse. It also
means ensuring the product falls in line with regulatory requirements for data server technology.
Dell also needs to put in place procedures for product recalls in the case of a manufacturing
defect. They most likely have something similar in place, so they need to ensure that the
node-level isolation system is included in that plan. They need to make it clear the responsibility
of all of the parties involved, such as manufacturers and suppliers. Dell also needs to consider
liability insurance in the case of unforeseen issues that may arise in order to protect them as a
company. In sum, thinking ahead of unforeseen circumstances and liabilities, and planning for
them will provide positive results for the implementation of our system for Dell.

4.4 Ethical Considerations
The nature of our project and product solution does not lend itself to a lot of ethical
considerations. A possible ethical dilemma would be in the case of human replacement. Anytime
a task is automated, it replaces the role that a human would be doing instead. In some cases, this
can cause people to lose their jobs due to the reduced need for labor in a facility. In our case, it
would be more likely for it to just ease the burden of work for the preexisting staff, and not cause
job replacement. It likely means that they will have more time to fix other issues instead of
tending to the request to remove a leaking server. There is also still the need to fix and replace
the server that was having issues, which still requires human resources. Another possible ethical
consideration is the possibility of injury. Anytime there is a mechanical assembly with moving
parts, there is the potential for human injury. Some possibilities would be a finger or hair stuck in
either the elevator or the actuator. To mitigate this issue, installing proper safety equipment
would be paramount. Some possible solutions would be light curtains installed on the back of the
server that would ensure the system would not operate if there is something obstructing the
space. Another possibility is installing sensors or cameras to detect people or obstructions in the
way and stop the system. Overall, there are very few ethical considerations, but the few that there
are can be overcome with preventive planning.

5.3 Technology Development Required and Future Work
1. Integration of Control System with Existing Rack Dashboard: The control system needs
to be integrated with the existing rack dashboard to allow for seamless user operation and
monitoring. System inputs and outputs should be mapped to the current dashboard
interface while maintaining ergonomic accessibility and minimizing any additional
complexity for operators.
2. Extension of Elevator to Reach All Rack Levels: The elevator mechanism should be
extended to reach all rack levels, ensuring full accessibility from top to bottom. The
prototype replicates all the dimensions of the existing rack apart from the 42U height.
3. Secure Mounting of the System to the Rack: The system needs to be securely mounted to
the rack to ensure stability during operation. Custom mounting brackets and fasteners
should be developed to minimize vibration, prevent shifting under load, and maintain
precise alignment over time.
4. Addition of Collars to Existing Quick Disconnects (QDs): Collars should be added to the
existing quick disconnects (QDs) to increase the surface area that the actuator interacts
with. The prototype uses shaft collars to achieve this goal but this is not a very reliable
solution and has a number of disadvantages. Incorporating this collar in the design of the
QD itself would solve most issues.
5. Reinforcement of Design for Longer Lifespan: The design should be reinforced to extend
the system’s operational lifespan. Stronger materials, improved structural support, and
better stress distribution strategies are recommended to enhance durability and reduce
maintenance needs over time.

6. Improvement of Assembly and Maintenance Processes: The design should be improved
to make assembly and maintenance easier. Simplifying component interfaces, using
standard fasteners, and designing for better service access will reduce installation time,
minimize errors, and facilitate quicker repairs and upgrades in the future. Some issues the
team recognized that require redesign are the motor housing, due to difficulty in
assembly, and the cantilever beam, which is difficult to maintain or replace.
5.4 Limitations of Solution
While the proposed design for the integration of the robotic tool and the rack system
offers significant advantages in terms of efficiency and functionality, several limitations and
considerations must be addressed to ensure seamless implementation and long-term reliability.
1. Limited Compatibility: The robotic tool system and its associated components may not be
universally compatible with all Dell server models or rack configurations. Different
server models may have variations in their design, such as rack spacing, connection
types, and internal configurations. This lack of uniformity can lead to challenges when
deploying the system across a diverse set of server environments. It is critical to assess
the specific models and configurations in use within the facility before implementing the
system. Compatibility testing and potential customization may be required for certain
configurations to ensure optimal performance.
2. Installation Complexity: The installation of the robotic tool and its integration with the
existing rack system might require a higher degree of technical expertise than initially
anticipated. The initial setup may involve manual adjustments, configuration of the
mechanical components, and calibration of the control systems. In addition, there may be
requirements for coordination with other parts of the server infrastructure, such as power
supply adjustments, wiring, and network integration. For a smooth deployment, thorough
training and preparation are essential for the installation team to minimize disruptions and
ensure the system is operational in a timely manner.
3. Mechanical Wear and Preventive Maintenance: As with any mechanical system, the
robotic tool will experience some level of mechanical wear over time. Moving parts and
actuators, in particular, are prone to stress, which can affect the system's performance and
longevity. It is important to incorporate the robotic tool into existing preventive
maintenance schedules to monitor and address wear and tear proactively. Regular
inspections, lubrication, and recalibration may be required to keep the system running
smoothly. An effective maintenance plan should be developed to ensure that downtime is
minimized and that the robotic tool continues to function efficiently throughout its
lifespan.
