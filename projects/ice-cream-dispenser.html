§ 01 · Overview What it is, and why a shop should care

The problem

Walk into a busy ice-cream shop and watch the back: someone is wrestling a 3-gallon bucket, dipping the scoop in warm water between pulls, eyeballing the size. The line builds, scoops shrink, the cost-per-cone walks away from the spreadsheet.

Industry kit either costs $8–15K for soft-serve machines (different product) or doesn’t exist at all for hard-pack at quick-serve scale.

The fix

A countertop or under-counter dispenser sized for hard-pack ice cream. The flavor sits in an insulated cartridge; a heated extrusion plate carves a fixed-volume puck on each lever pull; the puck drops into the cup or cone.

Nobody scoops. Nobody guesses. The first cone of the night looks like the thousandth.

§ 02 · Architecture Cartridge → form → drop
▪ COLD SIDE

Insulated cartridge

Holds a single flavor at −18 °C. Push-fit interface so a shop can swap flavors mid-shift in under 30 s.

→
▪ FORM

Heated extrusion head

Resistively-warmed nickel-plated face, geometry tuned for hard-pack. Cuts a clean puck without melt-back into the cartridge.

→
▪ ACTION

Lever & release

Manual lever drives a metered piston; release flap drops the puck into the receiving cup. No motors in the food path on V1.

§ 03 · Design rationale The decisions that shape the V1

Manual, not motorized (yet)

Motorizing the actuation is easy; certifying a motor in a food path is not. V1 uses a human lever for force—the part that’s hard is the thermal management of the cutting head, and that’s where the engineering goes.

Cartridges, not buckets

Standard 3-gallon tubs don’t pressure-seal and don’t have a clean push interface. A shop can buy or refill cartridges. Refill SKUs become a recurring revenue line.

Hard-pack, not soft-serve

Soft-serve is a solved (and saturated) market. Hard-pack at QSR scale isn’t. Aiming at the hard-pack shop is the right wedge.

Volume-metered, not weight-metered

A load cell in a freezing, sticky environment is a maintenance bill. Volume metering with a calibrated piston gets within ±5% at a fraction of the cost.

§ 04 · Open questions Known unknowns, in priority order
  • Cutting head temperature curve. Too cold = stalls. Too warm = melts the puck. Need to bench-sweep on real product, not water.
  • Cartridge seal & pressure tolerance. Has to handle a 30-lb push without rupturing the seal.
  • Cleaning cycle. Health-code compliant strip-down has to be <5 minutes.
  • Material food-safety. Need a 304 / 316 SS food path; head plating is the open spec.
  • Scoop "feel". A puck is shaped, not scooped. Will customers care? Field test required.
  • Refrigeration. V1 plugs into a chest freezer; V2 needs an integrated holdover. Cost balloons here.
