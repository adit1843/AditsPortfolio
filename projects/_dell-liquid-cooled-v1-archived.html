§ 01 · Overview What it is, and why it matters

The problem

High-density AI compute means racks now move several gallons per minute of dielectric or water-glycol coolant past hundreds of fittings. When a fitting fails, current practice is the same as it was for a server-room sprinkler: detect, sound the alarm, send a human, hope.

Single events have wiped out aisles of GPUs. Insurance pays out millions; the project schedule still slips by a quarter.

The fix

A distributed fluidic safety system sitting between the rack manifold and the loop—continuous flow + pressure + leak sensing, automated valve actuation, and a reroute path that keeps the rest of the aisle running while the affected rack is isolated.

End-to-end response time, leak to dry, target was 30 seconds. We hit 15.

§ 02 · Architecture Detect → decide → isolate → reroute
▪ INPUTS

Sensor array

Conductive leak strips at every coupling, plus inline flow + differential pressure sensors. Redundant paths so a single failure does not blind the system.

→
▪ DECIDE

Edge controller

Microcontroller-class node per rack, running a deterministic state machine. No cloud round-trip; loss of network does not stop isolation.

→
▪ ACT

Solenoid valves + bypass

Fail-safe normally-open isolation valves close in <3 s. A bypass loop keeps coolant flowing to neighboring racks during isolation.

▪ RECOVERY

Operator handoff

System pages on-call, surfaces the affected node, and waits for human acknowledgment before reopening flow. Every event is logged with timestamps and sensor traces for post-mortem.

§ 03 · Design rationale Decisions worth defending

Why edge, not centralized

A centralized controller would have been simpler to write, but a single network blip would suspend isolation across every rack. Each rack runs its own deterministic loop—the network only carries logs and operator messages.

Why redundant sensing

Conductive strips alone fail silent on dielectric coolants. Pressure delta alone false-fires on pump cycling. Together, voted, they cleared a 95% true-positive bar in 1,200+ seeded leak events.

Why fail-safe-open valves

Closing on power loss would kill compute every brownout. Open-on-fail keeps the GPUs running; a leak event has its own dedicated power and battery backup so the isolation actuator always has authority.

What we did not ship

An ML-based anomaly detector lived on the whiteboard for a month. Pulled it: deterministic thresholds were enough, and the certification story is far simpler.

§ 04 · Validation How we proved it
  • L11 lab validation at Dell’s Round Rock thermal lab. Full rack, real coolant, real heat load.
  • 1,200+ seeded leak events: pinhole, blown fitting, pump spike, slow weep. Detection 95%+ on each class.
  • Network-loss test: severed uplink mid-event. Local node still isolated within spec.
  • Long-run drift test: 30 days of coolant flow. Zero false positives after threshold calibration.
  • Patent application filed Q2 2025 (USPTO; patent pending).
  • Capstone Award of Excellence, Dwight Look College of Engineering.
  • Sponsor handoff: full BOM, control firmware, test harness, and operator UI delivered to Dell’s thermal-systems team.
  • NDA: deeper internals, schematics, and telemetry traces are not public—happy to walk through under one.
