§ 01
Context
Why build this at all
— THE BRIEF I GAVE MYSELF

Commercial smart locks have three problems: they phone home, they brick on low battery in a way that locks you out instead of leaving you a manual option, and their radios are mostly undocumented. I wanted the opposite — a lock that defaults to safe, that I can inspect, and that keeps working when the internet doesn’t.

Constraints were self-imposed:

  • Battery-powered door unit — no wires to route through a residential door.
  • Long-range radio — LoRa, not BLE. Works through walls, across the house, at milliwatts.
  • No vendor cloud — MQTT through a broker I control; the app is a hosted HTML file.
  • Mechanical override — the latch can still be turned by hand.

§ 03
Design Decisions
Why each part earned its place
— POWER BUDGET

The door unit had to live on four AAs for months, so everything revolves around sleeping hard. The ESP32 spends >99% of its life in deep sleep (~10 μA) and wakes on a GPIO interrupt from the LoRa module’s DIO pin.

The servo is the greedy one — a few hundred mA while moving. So I gate its 5 V rail with a logic-level P-channel MOSFET: the servo has zero power between moves and there’s no quiescent draw from a regulator.

— RADIO CHOICE

BLE would have been simpler but needs a phone in radio range. WiFi on the door means keeping the radio hot — not viable on batteries. LoRa lets the door unit wake, receive, ACK, and sleep again inside a budget of milliwatt-seconds.

Open problem: the wall unit uses an SX1262 (whatever Heltec shipped) and the door unit uses an SX1276. Both are LoRa-compatible in theory, but the syncword and modem parameters need to match exactly. This is the current bug I’m chasing.

— FAIL-SAFE BEHAVIOR

Three independent things confirm the lock state before the app shows “locked”:

  • Firmware internal state (what it thinks it did)
  • Limit-switch voltage read after the servo finishes
  • A round-trip ACK back up the chain

If any disagree, the UI goes yellow “unknown” and refuses to auto-lock. The deadbolt can still be turned by hand — the servo is back-drivable at rest.

§ 07
Lessons & Next
What I’d do differently on v2
— IN HINDSIGHT

Pick both radios from the same silicon family up front. Heltec was convenient for the wall unit but the SX1262/SX1276 mismatch cost me a week of oscilloscope time.

Do the power measurements before the mechanical design. I moved the battery holder twice because my first sleep-current numbers were wrong by an order of magnitude.

Ship the mechanical override even before ship. A smart lock that can’t be opened by hand is just an expensive paperweight the first time firmware crashes.
